  • 10+2 from a recognised board
  • Minimum 45% marks (40% for SC/ST/PwD)

  • Offline (pen and paper based)

  • English medium only
  • MCQs with 4 options each
  • ~25–30 reading passages total
🔍 What CLAT Actually Tests

CLAT is NOT a knowledge exam — it's an aptitude and comprehension exam. Every single question is passage-based. You don't need to memorise law — you need to read fast, reason carefully, and apply principles logically.

  • Read and understand dense passages of ~450 words very quickly
  • Apply rules/principles from a passage to new situations (not prior knowledge)
  • Identify conclusions, premises, and logical gaps in arguments
  • Interpret numerical data from text-based descriptions
  • Demonstrate current affairs awareness through passage comprehension

English Language

~22–26 questions • Passage-based comprehension

What to Expect Section A

3–4 passages of ~450 words each (fiction, non-fiction, journalistic). 5–8 questions follow each passage. No grammar fill-in-the-blanks or isolated vocabulary.

🎯 Main Idea / Central Theme

Ask: "What is the author's primary purpose in writing this?" The correct option will cover the whole passage — not just one paragraph. Avoid options that are too narrow (one example) or too broad (general topic).

🔍 Inference Questions

CLAT wants conservative inferences — only what is logically required by the passage. Don't bring in outside knowledge. The answer must be supportable by specific lines in the text. Keywords: "implies," "suggests," "can be inferred."

🎭 Author's Tone & Viewpoint

Common tones: critical, appreciative, neutral/objective, ironic, sarcastic, cautious, optimistic. Look for tone words — adjectives and verbs the author uses. Extreme tones ("furious," "ecstatic") are rarely correct in formal passages.

🔤 Meaning in Context

The question asks what a word/phrase means in this passage, not in general. Substitute each option into the sentence and pick the one that preserves the meaning. Don't rely on dictionary definitions.

✅ Fact vs. Opinion

Facts: verifiable, stated objectively. Opinions: subjective, debatable. Signal words for opinions: "should," "must," "believe," "argue," "ought to." CLAT often asks you to distinguish what the author claims vs. what the passage establishes.

💪 Strengthen/Weaken Questions

These appear in both English and Logical sections. To strengthen: find an option that supports the argument's key assumption or provides new evidence for it. To weaken: find an option that undermines the assumption or provides counter-evidence.

⚠️ These are the most common reasons students lose marks in the English section.

❌ "True but Not From Passage" Trap

An option may be factually true or seem reasonable, but if it's not supported by the passage, it's wrong. CLAT English is a closed-book exercise — only what the text says counts.

❌ "Extreme Language" Trap

Options with "always," "never," "only," "completely," "entirely," "must" are almost always wrong. Real-world arguments are rarely absolute. CLAT rewards nuanced, qualified answers.

❌ "Paraphrased Distractor" Trap

The wrong option rephrases something from the passage but subtly changes the meaning. Read very carefully — a small word like "not" or "only" can flip the meaning entirely.

❌ "Over-Inference" Trap

You might logically extend the author's argument beyond what they actually said. CLAT does not reward creative thinking here — stick to what's written, not what you think follows logically.

❌ First Paragraph Bias

Students often answer "main idea" questions based only on the first paragraph. The passage's core argument is often stated in the conclusion or developed throughout. Read the whole passage.

To render unclear or obscure; to confuse deliberately.

Overly concerned with minor details or rules; excessively academic.
Perfidious

💡 Don't memorise lists — instead, read these words in sentences. The context is what CLAT tests, not the dictionary definition.
⚡ The 5-Step Passage Attack Strategy

Step 1: Skim the questions first (60 sec)

Before reading the passage, scan all questions. This tells you what to look for — tone? main idea? specific inference? This saves 1–2 minutes per passage.

Step 2: Active reading (3 min)

Read with a purpose. Underline (mentally or on paper): the main claim, transitions ("however," "therefore," "despite"), and the conclusion. Don't re-read sentences.

Step 3: Answer structure questions first

Main idea, title, author's purpose — these rely on full passage understanding, so do them after reading. Fact-retrieval questions first (easiest, builds confidence).

Step 4: Eliminate, don't just identify

For tricky questions, eliminate wrong options first. Cross out extremes, off-topic options, and "true but not from passage" options. Pick the best remaining answer.

Step 5: Time check

Target 6–7 minutes per English passage. If stuck on a question for 90+ seconds, make your best guess and move on. Don't let one question derail the whole section.

✅ Daily habit: Read one Hindu/Indian Express editorial daily. After reading, ask yourself: What is the main argument? What evidence is given? What is the author's tone? This directly mirrors CLAT question types.

⚖️ Landmark Supreme Court Judgements
Kesavananda Bharati (1973)
Basic Structure Doctrine: Parliament can amend the Constitution but cannot alter its "basic structure" (federalism, secularism, democracy, judicial review).
ADM Jabalpur (1976)
Emergency & Habeas Corpus: SC held rights can be suspended during emergency (later overruled in Puttaswamy). Considered SC's darkest hour.
Maneka Gandhi (1978)
Expanded Art. 21: "Procedure established by law" must be fair, just, and reasonable — not arbitrary. Right to life is expansive.
Vishaka v. Rajasthan (1997)
Sexual Harassment: Laid down guidelines for workplaces until Parliament enacted the POSH Act (2013).
S.R. Bommai (1994)
President's Rule: SC can review imposition of Art. 356. Floor test before dismissal of state govt. federalism strengthened.
Indra Sawhney (1992)
OBC Reservations: Upheld 27% OBC quota. Set 50% ceiling on total reservations. Creamy layer exclusion mandated.
K.S. Puttaswamy (2017)
Right to Privacy: Privacy is a fundamental right under Art. 21. Overruled ADM Jabalpur. Basis for data protection law.
Navtej Singh Johar (2018)
Section 377: Decriminalised consensual same-sex relations among adults. Overruled Suresh Kumar Koushal (2013).
Joseph Shine (2018)
Adultery (Sec. 497): Struck down as unconstitutional. Treated women as husband's property. Violates dignity and equality.
Electoral Bonds (2024)
Political Funding: SC struck down Electoral Bond Scheme as unconstitutional — violated voters' right to information under Art. 19(1)(a).
📜 Key Constitutional Articles
Preamble
Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic. Justice, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity. Added "Socialist" and "Secular" by 42nd Amendment (1976).
Art. 12–35
Part III — Fundamental Rights. Enforceable in courts. Art. 13: Laws inconsistent with FRs are void.
Art. 14
Right to Equality: Equal before law + equal protection of laws. Allows reasonable classification (not arbitrary).
Art. 19
6 freedoms: speech/expression, assembly, association, movement, residence, profession. All subject to reasonable restrictions.
Art. 21
Right to Life and Personal Liberty. Most expansive FR — includes privacy, livelihood, education, health, dignity.
Art. 21A
Right to Education (added by 86th Amendment, 2002). Free and compulsory education for 6–14 years. → Led to RTE Act 2009.
Art. 32
Right to Constitutional Remedies. Dr. Ambedkar: "Heart and soul of the Constitution." Writs: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Certiorari, Quo Warranto.
Art. 36–51 (DPSP)
Directive Principles — non-justiciable (cannot be enforced in court) but fundamental to governance. Art. 44 = Uniform Civil Code; Art. 45 = Early childhood care; Art. 48A = Environment protection.
Art. 51A
11 Fundamental Duties (added by 42nd Amendment). Non-justiciable but used by courts to interpret laws.
Art. 368
Amendment procedure. Parliament can amend but not destroy Basic Structure (Kesavananda Bharati).

Legal Reasoning

~28–32 questions • No prior law knowledge required

✅ Great news: You do NOT need to know law to ace this section. All legal principles are provided in the passage. Your job is to apply the rule to the facts — exactly like a lawyer using IRAC.
The IRAC Method Master This

I Issue — What legal question needs to be answered?

Identify what is being asked. Is it about liability? Rights? Validity of contract? The issue frames your entire analysis.

R Rule — What principle is stated in the passage?

Extract the exact legal rule from the passage. Do not use your own knowledge. Even if you know real law differs, use ONLY the passage's rule.

A Application — Does the rule cover these facts?

Match each element of the rule to the facts. If the rule requires three conditions, check each condition against the facts. Apply mechanically, not intuitively.

C Conclusion — What does the application yield?

State the answer clearly: liable / not liable, valid / invalid, guilty / not guilty. Pick the option that matches your IRAC conclusion.

🚫 Biggest mistake: Applying real-world legal knowledge instead of the passage rule. If the passage says "an agreement made on a Thursday is invalid," accept it — don't bring in contract law knowledge.
Essential Legal Terms for CLAT
Tort
A civil wrong causing harm, giving the victim the right to sue for compensation. Not a crime. Examples: negligence, defamation, nuisance, trespass.
Negligence
Failure to take reasonable care resulting in damage. Three elements: (1) Duty of care owed, (2) Breach of that duty, (3) Damage caused by the breach.
Mens Rea
"Guilty mind" — criminal intent. Most crimes need both act (Actus Reus) AND intent. Without mens rea, usually no criminal liability.
Actus Reus
"Guilty act" — the physical element of a crime. A voluntary wrongful action or forbidden omission.
Consideration
Something of value exchanged in a contract. "Past consideration is no consideration." Without consideration, a contract is not enforceable (with exceptions).
Offer & Acceptance
Essential elements of a contract. Counter-offer = rejection of original + new offer. Acceptance must be unconditional and communicated.
Strict Liability
Liability without proof of fault. For inherently dangerous activities. Based on Rylands v. Fletcher — "a person who brings onto land something likely to do mischief is strictly liable."
Vicarious Liability
Holding A liable for acts of B. Common example: employer for employee's acts done in the course of employment.
Volenti Non Fit Injuria
"No injury to one who consents." Complete defence to tort claims. E.g., a boxer consents to being punched in a match.
Res Ipsa Loquitur
"The thing speaks for itself." When the facts so clearly indicate negligence that no further proof is needed. Burden shifts to defendant.
Judicial Review
Power of courts to review constitutionality of laws and government actions. A basic structure feature of Indian Constitution.
Locus Standi
Legal standing to sue — the right to bring a case before a court. Relaxed in India through PIL (Public Interest Litigation).

🔴 Law of Torts — Key Principles

  • Defamation: False statement lowering reputation. Libel (written) vs. Slander (spoken). Truth is a complete defence.
  • Nuisance: Private nuisance = unreasonable interference with use/enjoyment of land. Public nuisance = affects community.
  • Trespass: To person (battery, assault), to land (entering without permission), to goods.
  • Absolute Liability (India): MC Mehta v. Union of India — no exceptions for hazardous industry causing harm. Stricter than Rylands.

📝 Law of Contracts — Essentials

  • Valid contract needs: Offer + Acceptance + Consideration + Capacity + Free consent + Lawful object
  • Void contract: Never had legal effect (e.g., by minor)
  • Voidable contract: Valid until challenged (e.g., obtained by fraud/coercion)
  • Free consent lacks when: Coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake
  • Invitation to treat: NOT an offer — just an invitation to make offers (e.g., shop display, advertisement)

⚖️ Criminal Law Basics

  • IPC (now BNS): Governs substantive criminal law in India
  • Culpable Homicide vs. Murder: Murder = intention to kill + no exception. Culpable Homicide = killing without full murder intent.
  • General Exceptions: Private defence, necessity, consent, accident, insanity, intoxication
  • Abetment: Instigating or assisting someone to commit a crime = also criminal liability
📜 The Five Constitutional Writs
Habeas Corpus
"Have the body." Challenges unlawful detention — requires the detaining authority to produce the person before a court and justify detention. Available under Art. 32 (SC) and Art. 226 (HC).
Mandamus
"We command." Orders a public official or body to perform a duty they are legally required to do. Cannot be issued against President/Governor or private individuals generally.
Certiorari
"To be certified." Issued by superior court to quash (cancel) the order of a lower court/tribunal that acted beyond its jurisdiction or violated natural justice.
Prohibition
Prevents a lower court/tribunal from exceeding its jurisdiction. Issued BEFORE an inferior court makes a final decision. Preventive in nature (unlike Certiorari which is curative).
Quo Warranto
"By what authority?" Challenges someone's right to hold a public office. Requires them to prove legal authority to exercise the office.
Memory trick — HMCPQ: "Habeas Mandamus Certiorari Prohibition Quo Warranto" → "Huge Mango Cakes Please, Quickly!"

Logical Reasoning

~22–26 questions • Argument analysis from passages

💡 CLAT Logical ≠ Puzzles. NO syllogisms, blood relations, seating arrangements, or coding-decoding. CLAT only tests argument analysis from written passages. Practice by reading op-ed columns critically.

📌 Deductive Arguments

Conclusion necessarily follows from premises. If premises are true, the conclusion MUST be true. Example: "All cats are mammals. Whiskers is a cat. Therefore Whiskers is a mammal." Focus: Is the argument valid (properly structured)?

📌 Inductive Arguments

Conclusion probably follows from premises — evidence supports but doesn't guarantee. Example: "The last 10 swans I saw were white. Therefore all swans are white." Focus: Is the argument strong (is the evidence sufficient)?

📌 Abductive Arguments

"Best explanation" reasoning — inferring the most likely cause from observed effects. Example: "The floor is wet. It probably rained, or someone spilled water." Used in legal reasoning to establish facts.

🔑 Identifying an Argument's Structure

In any passage, identify:

  • Conclusion: The main claim being made. Signal words: "therefore," "thus," "hence," "so," "consequently."
  • Premises: The evidence/reasons. Signal words: "because," "since," "given that," "as," "for."
  • Assumption: Unstated premise the argument depends on — never explicitly stated but must be true for conclusion to hold.

🔵 Strengthen Questions

Find the option that makes the conclusion MORE likely. Usually adds new evidence supporting the argument's key assumption, or eliminates an alternative explanation. Ask: "Which option, if true, would make the argument stronger?"

🔴 Weaken Questions

Find the option that attacks the argument's assumption or provides counter-evidence. Doesn't have to destroy the argument — just make it less convincing. Most common CLAT question type.

🟡 Assumption Questions

The assumption is the hidden premise without which the argument breaks down. Use the Negation Test: negate each option. If negating it destroys the argument, that's the assumption.

🟢 Inference Questions

What can be concluded from the passage? Must be necessarily true given the passage — not just possibly true. More conservative than "what might be inferred."

🟣 Flaw Questions

What logical error does the argument commit? Common flaws: hasty generalisation, correlation-causation confusion, false analogy, circular reasoning, ad hominem. Name the flaw type.

⚪ Parallel Reasoning

Find the option that follows the same logical structure as the original argument — regardless of subject matter. Focus on the form/pattern, not the content.

Common Logical Fallacies
Ad Hominem
Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. "You can't trust her opinion on healthcare — she's not a doctor."
Straw Man
Misrepresenting an opponent's argument in a weaker form to more easily attack it.
False Dichotomy
Presenting only two options when more exist. "Either you support this policy or you don't care about children."
Slippery Slope
Claiming one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without justification. "If we legalise X, soon everything will be legal."
Correlation ≠ Causation
Two things occurring together doesn't mean one caused the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer — they don't cause each other.
Hasty Generalisation
Broad conclusion from too small or unrepresentative a sample. "I met two rude people from Delhi. People from Delhi are rude."
Circular Reasoning
The conclusion is used as a premise. "The law is just because it is the law." The argument assumes what it's trying to prove.
Appeal to Authority
Claiming something is true because an authority (not necessarily expert on this topic) said so.
Red Herring
Introducing an irrelevant topic to distract from the main issue.

Quantitative Techniques

~10–14 questions • Class 10 maths only

📊 Strategy: Only 10–14 questions but highly scoring — all Class 10 level. No calculus, trigonometry, or complex algebra. Aim for 80%+ accuracy. The hard part isn't the math — it's reading and extracting numbers from text.

📊 Percentages

  • Percentage increase: [(New − Old) / Old] × 100
  • Percentage decrease: [(Old − New) / Old] × 100
  • X% of Y = (X/100) × Y
  • Most common in data interpretation: "what percentage is X of total Y?"

⚖️ Ratio & Proportion

  • Ratio a:b means a/(a+b) and b/(a+b) of total respectively
  • If a:b = 2:3 and total = 50, then a = 20, b = 30
  • Compound ratio: multiply ratios; Inverse ratio: flip them

📈 Averages & Weighted Averages

  • Simple average = sum / count
  • Weighted average = (sum of value × weight) / sum of weights
  • If average changes when one item is added — use algebra

💰 Profit, Loss & Interest

  • Profit% = (SP − CP)/CP × 100 | Loss% = (CP − SP)/CP × 100
  • Simple Interest = PRT/100 (P=principal, R=rate, T=time in years)
  • Compound Interest = P[(1 + R/100)ⁿ − 1]
  • Effective rate: for CI, always slightly higher than SI

📉 Data Interpretation

  • Extract the right numbers from the passage — don't rush
  • Common formats: comparison tables, growth data, budget allocations
  • Check units carefully (crores vs. lakhs, % vs. absolute numbers)
  • Estimate when exact calculation is slow — CLAT doesn't need 5-decimal precision
All Formulas to Memorise

Percentage

% Change = [(New − Old) / Old] × 100  |  X is what % of Y = (X/Y) × 100

Profit & Loss

SP = CP × (1 + P%/100)  |  CP = SP / (1 + P%/100)  |  Discount = MP − SP  |  Discount% = (Discount/MP) × 100

Interest

SI = PRT/100  |  A (SI) = P + PRT/100  |  CI = P[(1+r/100)ⁿ − 1]  |  A (CI) = P(1+r/100)ⁿ

Averages

Mean = ΣX / n  |  If avg of n items is A, and one item X is removed, new avg = (nA − X)/(n−1)

Speed, Distance, Time

D = S × T  |  Average speed = 2S₁S₂/(S₁+S₂) for equal distances  |  Time = D/S

Ratio Tips

If A:B = m:n, then A = m/(m+n) × Total  |  If A:B = 2:3, B:C = 4:5, then A:B:C = 8:12:15

⚡ High-Impact Tips
  • Read twice, calculate once: Most errors come from misreading the data, not wrong math.
  • Estimate aggressively: For MCQs, approximate calculations to 2 significant figures. If options are far apart, rounding works fine.
  • Check units: Watch out for lakhs vs. crores, per annum vs. total, etc.
  • No calculator: Practice mental math — particularly mental percentages (e.g., 17.5% = 10% + 5% + 2.5%)
  • Spend time here wisely: This section has the fewest questions. Aim for 8–10 minutes total. Don't let one DI question eat 4 minutes.
  • Do this section near the end: Fresh eyes for calculation reduce errors.

Preparation Strategy

Month-by-month plan for Class 12 students targeting CLAT 2027

The Golden Rules of CLAT Prep
  • Read daily without exception. The Hindu editorial + news = your CLAT foundation. This is non-negotiable.
  • Passage practice beats rote learning. CLAT rewards comprehension and application, not memorisation.
  • Start mock tests 6 months before exam. The habit of timed, pressured practice is irreplaceable.
  • Analyse WHY you got something wrong, not just that you did. Pattern recognition in your errors is the key to improvement.
  • Balance Class 12 boards with CLAT. You need 45% in boards for eligibility — and college performance matters for law school applications.
  • GK is built daily, not in a week. Start now. No amount of last-minute cramming can substitute for 12 months of reading.

📅 Phase 1: Foundation (Apr – Jun 2026)

▼
  • Start The Hindu daily — editorial + national/international pages. This is your single most important CLAT habit.
  • Complete NCERT Class 10 Maths — percentages, ratios, averages, SI/CI. 2 weeks is enough.
  • Read NCERT Class 11 Political Science (Indian Constitution at Work) — covers Fundamental Rights, DPSPs, Parliament, Judiciary.
  • Start a Current Affairs Diary — note 5 key news items daily. Review every Sunday.
  • Read 1–2 official CLAT sample papers to understand format and passage length.
  • Begin vocabulary building — 10 new words/week from editorial reading (in context, not lists).
  • Watch free YouTube orientation videos: CLAT Possible, iQuanta Law, LexiConn.

📅 Phase 2: Concept Building (Jul – Sep 2026)

▼
  • Deep-dive into Legal Reasoning — practice IRAC daily. Solve 2–3 passages per day from past papers.
  • Study logical fallacies and argument structure — read op-ed columns critically (2–3 hrs/week).
  • Solve Reading Comprehension passages daily — time yourself at 5–6 minutes per passage.
  • Compile Landmark Judgements notes (use the GK section of this guide).
  • Start past CLAT papers (2020–2025) — one full paper per week. Analyse errors by section.
  • Build Static GK notes: Constitutional Articles, Indian Geography, National Symbols, World Organisations.
  • Continue daily newspaper reading; add Indian Express Explained section.

📅 Phase 3: Intensive Practice (Oct – Nov 2026)

▼
  • Give 2 full mock tests per week under strict timed conditions (120 minutes, offline).
  • After each mock — deep error analysis by section. Identify your 3 weakest areas and address them immediately.
  • Revise all landmark judgements, constitutional articles, and legal terms (use flashcards from this guide).
  • Compile last 18 months' CA into a quick-revision booklet — one page per month.
  • Track your reading speed: aim for 200+ words per minute for CLAT passages.
  • Solve all past CLAT papers 2018–2025. Identify recurring question types.
  • Attempt AILET and SLAT mock papers for additional exposure.

📅 Phase 4: Revision & Mastery (Dec 2026)

▼
  • Give a mock test every 2 days. Focus on time management and accuracy, not new learning.
  • Only revise — no new topics in the last 3 weeks before the exam.
  • Review CA booklet daily in the final 2 weeks.
  • Do sectional tests for Legal Reasoning and GK (your highest-weightage sections).
  • Maintain sleep schedule and physical routine. Cognitive performance degrades with sleep deprivation.
  • Re-read all notes: legal terms, constitutional articles, landmark judgements.
📅 Ideal Daily Schedule (2.5 hrs alongside Class 12)

Morning — 30 mins — Newspaper Reading

The Hindu editorial + 2 key news stories. Add to CA diary. This builds GK, vocabulary, and reading speed simultaneously.

After School/College — 60 mins — Section Practice

Mon/Wed = Legal Reasoning passages  |  Tue/Thu = Logical Reasoning  |  Fri = English RC (timed)  |  Sat = GK revision + CA quiz  |  Sun = Full mock or past paper

Evening — 30 mins — Quant + Vocab

10 min: 3–4 Quant problems  |  10 min: vocabulary review (from editorial)  |  10 min: legal terms or flashcards

Weekend — 2–3 hours — Mock or Deep Sectional

One full mock test OR two full-section practices. Always follow with a written error analysis. What type of question did you get wrong? Why?

🏁 Exam Day Attempt Strategy
  • Recommended order: Legal Reasoning → English → GK → Logical → Quant
  • Approximate time: Legal 28 min → English 20 min → GK 25 min → Logical 20 min → Quant 10 min → Review 17 min
  • Skip-and-return rule: Stuck on a question for 90 seconds? Mark it and move on. Don't let one question cost three marks elsewhere.
  • Negative marking threshold: Only attempt if you can eliminate at least 2 options. Skip if genuinely unsure — −0.25 hurts.
  • Keep 10–15 minutes at end to return to flagged questions. Never end the exam early.
  • Mental reset between passages: Take one deep breath before each new passage. Carry over comprehension from previous passage will mislead you.

AILET 2026English — Reading ComprehensionQ1
The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into law enforcement practices has sparked a global debate on ethics, privacy, and accountability. From facial recognition systems in public spaces to predictive policing algorithms, AI is transforming how crimes are detected and prevented. However, the benefits of efficiency and data-driven decision-making are accompanied by concerns about fairness, bias, and the potential erosion of civil liberties. Predictive policing uses historical crime data to forecast where crimes are likely to occur or who might commit them. Proponents argue that this allows police to allocate resources more efficiently and reduce crime rates. However, critics warn that such systems may reinforce existing societal biases. The lack of transparency in how these systems operate — the "Black Box" problem — makes it difficult to challenge wrongful predictions or decisions in a court of law.
1. What is the central concern raised in the passage regarding AI in law enforcement?
A. Replacing human officers
B. High operational costs
C. Reinforcing bias and reducing fairness
D. Inability to analyze real-time data
✅ Answer: C — The passage repeatedly highlights concerns about fairness, bias, and erosion of civil liberties. Predictive policing and FRT are criticised mainly because they may perpetuate injustice against marginalized communities. Bias and fairness are the core concerns.

AILET 2026Logical Reasoning — SyllogismQ84
Shopkeepers are Big. Some Big persons violate night curfew. All who violate night curfew are fined. Anil is Big.
Examine the following statements: I. Anil is a Shopkeeper II. Anil may violate a night curfew III. Anil will definitely violate night curfew IV. If Anil is a shopkeeper, he will get fined V. Anil will definitely get fined. Select the correct option:
A. Only I, III and IV
B. Only II
C. Only I and III
D. Only I and V
✅ Answer: B — Only II is true. I is false (all shopkeepers are big, but not all big persons are shopkeepers). II is true (some big persons violate curfew; since Anil is big, he MAY violate). III is false (only SOME big persons violate — not certainty). IV is false (shopkeeper → big → may violate → may be fined, not definite). V is false for same reason.
AILET 2026Logical Reasoning — Principle-FactQ86
Principle: A driver is responsible for controlling their vehicle and avoiding harm to pedestrians, unless the pedestrian acted recklessly or unlawfully. Facts: A driver hits a pedestrian who suddenly runs across the road outside a crosswalk without looking. The driver was obeying all traffic rules.
Which of the following is correct?
A. The driver is liable for the pedestrian's injuries.
B. The pedestrian's reckless action absolves the driver of liability.
C. Both the driver and pedestrian share liability equally.
D. The driver is always liable in pedestrian accidents.
✅ Answer: B — The principle has an exception: if the pedestrian acted recklessly or unlawfully. Running across the road outside a crosswalk without looking = reckless/unlawful behaviour. The exception applies → driver not liable. Option D contradicts the principle's own exception clause.
AILET 2026Logical Reasoning — Principle-FactQ90
Principle: A public official must avoid conflicts of interest that could undermine public trust, even if no actual wrongdoing occurs. Facts: A city mayor awards a major construction contract to a company owned by her cousin. She discloses the relationship publicly and follows all procurement procedures.
Apply the principle and select the most appropriate option:
A. Contract should proceed since procedures were followed.
B. Contract should be avoided to maintain public trust.
C. Cousin should sell the company before bidding.
D. Mayor should resign.
✅ Answer: B — The principle says avoid conflicts that "could undermine public trust" — even with no actual wrongdoing. Disclosing and following procedures doesn't eliminate the conflict of interest itself. The appearance of a conflict is sufficient under this principle. The contract should be avoided. Option A ignores the conflict-of-interest aspect. Option D is too extreme — the principle doesn't require resignation.
