🚫 Negative Marking: −0.25 marks for every wrong answer. Avoid blind guessing — only attempt if you can eliminate 2+ options.

Eligibility

  • 10+2 or equivalent from a recognised board
  • Minimum 45% marks (40% for SC/ST/PwD)
  • No upper age limit
  • Appearing students in Class 12 are also eligible

Exam Format

  • Offline (pen and paper)
  • Duration: 2 hours
  • Language: English only
  • MCQs — 4 options each
  • Reading-intensive — 25-30 passages!
What CLAT Actually Tests 🔍

CLAT is NOT a knowledge exam — it's an aptitude and comprehension exam. All questions are passage-based. You don't need to memorise law, but you need to read fast and reason well.

  • Read & understand dense passages quickly (450 words each)
  • Apply principles from the passage to new situations
  • Identify arguments, draw inferences, spot logical gaps
  • Interpret numerical data from text (not formula-based)
  • Demonstrate awareness of current events through passage comprehension

English Language

~22–26 questions • Passage-based comprehension

What to Expect Section A

Passages of ~450 words from fiction, non-fiction, and journalistic writing. Questions test comprehension, inference, vocabulary-in-context, and the ability to summarise.

  • 3–4 passages per section
  • Each followed by 5–8 questions
  • Literary and journalistic texts (both contemporary & historical)
  • No fill-in-the-blank grammar or isolated vocabulary questions
Key Skills to Develop
  • Main Idea / Central Theme — What is the passage fundamentally about?
  • Inference Questions — What can be logically concluded even if not stated directly?
  • Author's Tone & Viewpoint — Is the author critical, appreciative, neutral, ironic?
  • Meaning in Context — What does this word/phrase mean HERE, not in the dictionary?
  • Fact vs. Opinion — Distinguish stated facts from the author's opinions
  • Summarisation — Choose the option that best captures the passage's essence
  • Strengthens / Weakens — Which option supports or challenges the passage's argument?
💡 Pro Tip: Read the questions FIRST before reading the passage. This helps you read purposefully and locate answers faster. You have roughly 5–7 minutes per passage.
Daily Practice Routine
  • Read The Hindu or The Indian Express editorial every day — these match CLAT's passage style perfectly
  • Read 1 short story or essay every week (try Ruskin Bond, R.K. Narayan, or short essays by Orwell)
  • After reading, summarise the passage in 2 sentences — this sharpens comprehension
  • Note down unfamiliar words and learn them in context (not isolated flashcards)
  • Practice timed reading: aim to read 450 words in under 4 minutes
Common Traps in English Section ⚠️
  • "Extreme" answer choices — options using "always," "never," "completely" are usually wrong
  • True but not from passage — the answer must be supported by the passage, not general knowledge
  • Paraphrased distractor — the passage may say X; the wrong option rephrases it slightly but changes the meaning
  • Out-of-scope inferences — don't overinfer; CLAT wants logical, conservative inferences

Current Affairs & General Knowledge

~28–32 questions • Highest weightage section

🔥 This is the largest section in CLAT — approximately 28–32 questions. It is also the section where consistent daily reading gives the biggest advantage over last-minute studying.
What to Expect Section B
  • Passage from a news article or journalistic source (~450 words)
  • Questions test whether you understood the passage AND have contextual current affairs awareness
  • Covers: national events, international affairs, judiciary, economy, science & technology, arts & culture
  • Static GK (historical events, constitution basics, geography) may be tested in context
Topics to Cover

CURRENT AFFAIRS (Last 18 months)

  • Major Supreme Court & High Court judgements
  • Central government policies & schemes
  • International relations (India's bilateral ties)
  • Economic developments (Budget, RBI policies)
  • Science, space (ISRO), environment & climate
  • Awards, appointments, obituaries
  • Sports: Olympics, Cricket World Cup, etc.

STATIC GK

  • Indian Constitution: Preamble, Fundamental Rights, DPSPs
  • Indian History: Freedom movement, key events
  • Indian Geography: Rivers, states, capitals
  • National symbols, days, government bodies
  • World organisations: UN, WTO, IMF, World Bank
  • Arts & Literature: Booker Prize, Nobel, etc.
Landmark Judgements to Know ⚖️
  • Kesavananda Bharati (1973) — Basic Structure Doctrine of Constitution
  • Maneka Gandhi (1978) — Expanded scope of Article 21 (Right to Life)
  • Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997) — Sexual harassment at workplace guidelines
  • S.R. Bommai (1994) — Restrictions on misuse of President's Rule (Art. 356)
  • Navtej Singh Johar (2018) — Decriminalised consensual same-sex relations (Section 377)
  • Joseph Shine (2018) — Struck down adultery law (Section 497)
  • Justice K.S. Puttaswamy (2017) — Right to Privacy as Fundamental Right
  • Indra Sawhney (1992) — OBC reservations, 50% cap on reservations
Key Constitutional Articles to Remember
  • Art. 12–35: Fundamental Rights
  • Art. 14: Right to Equality
  • Art. 19: Freedom of Speech, etc.
  • Art. 21: Right to Life & Personal Liberty
  • Art. 21A: Right to Education
  • Art. 25–28: Freedom of Religion
  • Art. 32: Right to Constitutional Remedies (Dr. Ambedkar called it "Heart of Constitution")
  • Art. 44: Uniform Civil Code (DPSP)
  • Art. 51A: Fundamental Duties
  • Art. 368: Amendment procedure
  • Art. 356: President's Rule in States
💡 Best Strategy: Read The Hindu daily. Maintain a monthly current affairs notebook. Review it every Sunday. Use Legal Bites Monthly CA PDF or Drishti IAS Current Affairs for compilation. Cover the last 12–18 months before the exam.

Legal Reasoning

~28–32 questions • No prior law knowledge needed

✅ Good news: You do NOT need to have studied law to score well here. All legal principles are given in the passage. Your job is to read, extract the rule, and apply it to the fact situation — just like a lawyer would!
How Legal Reasoning Works IRAC Method

Every legal reasoning question follows a PRINCIPLE → FACTS → APPLICATION structure. Learn the IRAC approach:

  • I — Issue: What legal question is being asked?
  • R — Rule: What principle/rule is given in the passage?
  • A — Application: How does the rule apply to the facts given?
  • C — Conclusion: What is the answer based on this application?
⚠️ Apply ONLY the rule stated in the passage — do NOT use your own legal knowledge or common sense if it contradicts the passage.
Important Legal Concepts (With Definitions)
Tort
A civil wrong (not a crime) that causes harm to someone, giving them the right to sue for compensation. E.g., negligence, defamation, trespass.
Negligence
Failure to take reasonable care, resulting in damage to another. Elements: Duty of care + Breach + Damage caused by breach.
Offer & Acceptance
A valid contract requires a clear offer by one party and unconditional acceptance by the other. Counter-offer = rejection of original offer.
Consideration
Something of value exchanged between parties to a contract. "Past consideration is no consideration."
Mens Rea
The "guilty mind" — criminal intent. Most crimes require both act (Actus Reus) AND intent (Mens Rea).
Strict Liability
Liability without proof of fault/negligence. Used for inherently dangerous activities (e.g., Rylands v. Fletcher rule).
Vicarious Liability
When one person is held liable for the acts of another (e.g., employer for employee's acts during employment).
Volenti Non Fit Injuria
Consent is a complete defence — "No injury is done to one who consents." E.g., a boxer cannot sue for injuries in a match.
Habeas Corpus
A writ requiring a person under arrest to be brought before a judge. Protects against unlawful detention. Filed under Art. 32 or Art. 226.
Mandamus
A writ directing a public official/body to perform a duty they are legally obligated to perform.
Certiorari
Writ to quash (cancel) the order of a lower court/tribunal that has acted beyond its jurisdiction.
Judicial Review
Power of courts to review constitutionality of laws or government actions. A basic feature of India's Constitution.
Common Legal Reasoning Topics in CLAT
  • Law of Contracts
  • Torts (Negligence, Defamation, Nuisance)
  • Criminal Law basics (IPC concepts)
  • Constitutional Law (Fundamental Rights)
  • Property Law concepts
  • Family Law (brief scenarios)
  • Public policy & moral philosophy
  • Environmental law scenarios

Logical Reasoning

~22–26 questions • Argument analysis from passages

What the Section Tests Section D

Like Legal Reasoning, this is passage-based. Each passage contains an argument. You must analyse it critically.

  • Identify the conclusion of the argument
  • Identify the premises (evidence/reasons given)
  • Find assumptions the argument depends on
  • Determine what weakens or strengthens the argument
  • Detect logical flaws or fallacies in reasoning
  • Apply the pattern of reasoning to a new situation (analogical reasoning)
Core Logical Reasoning Concepts

📌 Types of Arguments

  • Deductive: Conclusion necessarily follows from premises. If premises are true, conclusion MUST be true.
  • Inductive: Conclusion probably follows from premises. Evidence supports but doesn't guarantee conclusion.
  • Abductive: "Best explanation" reasoning — concludes the most likely cause.

📌 Question Types

  • Strengthen: Which option makes the conclusion MORE likely to be true?
  • Weaken: Which option makes the conclusion LESS likely / introduces a flaw?
  • Assumption: What MUST be assumed for the argument to hold?
  • Inference: What can be logically derived from the passage?
  • Flaw: What logical error does the argument commit?
  • Parallel Reasoning: Which option follows the same logical structure?
Common Logical Fallacies to Spot
Ad Hominem
Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.
Straw Man
Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack.
False Dichotomy
Presenting only two options when more exist. "You're either with us or against us."
Slippery Slope
Claiming one event will lead to an extreme negative consequence without justification.
Correlation ≠ Causation
Just because two things happen together doesn't mean one caused the other.
Hasty Generalisation
Drawing a broad conclusion from too small or unrepresentative a sample.
Circular Reasoning
The conclusion is used as a premise. "X is true because X is true."
💡 CLAT Logical Reasoning ≠ Puzzles. Unlike many other exams, CLAT does NOT test syllogisms, blood relations, seating arrangement, or coding-decoding. It is purely about analysing written arguments. Practice by reading op-ed columns and asking: "What is the author's main claim? What evidence do they give? What are they assuming?"

Quantitative Techniques

~10–14 questions • Lowest weightage, but scoring

📊 Strategy Note: This section has the fewest questions but is very manageable. It only requires Class 10 maths — no calculus, trigonometry, or complex algebra. Aim for 80%+ accuracy here as these are the most straightforward marks to score.
Format & What to Expect Section E
  • Short passages presenting numerical data (tables, graphs described in text, or word problems)
  • 2–4 questions follow each passage
  • You need to extract numbers from the text, then apply mathematical operations
  • Calculator NOT allowed — practice mental math
Topics Covered (Class 10 Level)
  • Percentages — increase/decrease, percentage of a total
  • Ratio & Proportion — comparing quantities, direct/inverse
  • Averages — mean, weighted average
  • Profit & Loss — cost price, selling price, % profit/loss
  • Simple & Compound Interest
  • Basic Algebra — solving simple equations
  • Data Interpretation — reading tables, charts in text form
  • Mensuration basics — area, perimeter (rarely tested)
  • Statistical Estimation — median, mode (basic)
  • Time & Work / Speed basics
Key Formulas to Memorise
  • Percentage Change = [(New − Old) / Old] × 100
  • Profit % = [(SP − CP) / CP] × 100
  • Simple Interest = (P × R × T) / 100
  • Compound Interest = P[(1 + R/100)ⁿ − 1]
  • Average = Sum of all values / Number of values
  • Ratio a:b means for every 'a' units of X, there are 'b' units of Y
💡 Practice extracting numbers from a paragraph and then solving — the challenge is not the maths, it's reading carefully enough to find the right numbers.

Preparation Strategy

Month-by-month plan for Class 12 students

Assumption: Your friend is in Class 12 (2025–26) and will appear for CLAT UG 2027 (December 2026). That gives approximately 12–18 months — which is excellent time to prepare!
The Golden Rules of CLAT Preparation
  • Read every day — No shortcut. The Hindu editorial + any quality book = CLAT foundation
  • Passage practice > rote learning — CLAT rewards comprehension, not memorisation
  • Mock tests are non-negotiable — Start giving mocks 6 months before the exam
  • Analyse mistakes, don't just count them — WHY you got it wrong matters more than THAT you got it wrong
  • Balance board exam prep with CLAT — Class 12 marks also matter for eligibility (45% cutoff)
  • Don't ignore GK/CA — It's the biggest section; build the habit early

📅 Phase 1: Foundation (Apr – Jun 2026)

▼
  • Start reading The Hindu daily — focus on editorial and national pages
  • Complete NCERT Class 10 Maths for Quant basics (revisit percentages, ratios, etc.)
  • Read about the Indian Constitution — Preamble, Fundamental Rights, DPSPs (NCERT Class 11 Political Science)
  • Start a CA notebook — note down 5 key news items daily
  • Read 1–2 CLAT sample papers to understand passage format
  • Begin vocabulary building — 10 new words/week from editorial reading
  • Watch CLAT orientation videos on YouTube (Clat Possible, iQuanta Law)

📅 Phase 2: Concept Building (Jul – Sep 2026)

▼
  • Deep-dive into Legal Reasoning — practice IRAC method daily using past CLAT passages
  • Study logical fallacies and argument structures (2–3 hours/week)
  • Practice Reading Comprehension passages daily — time yourself (5 min per passage)
  • Compile landmark judgements notes (see GK section above)
  • Start past year CLAT papers (2020–2025) — do one per week
  • Continue daily newspaper reading, now adding Indian Express Explained section
  • Review CA notebook every Sunday

📅 Phase 3: Intensive Practice (Oct – Nov 2026)

▼
  • Give 2 full mock tests per week (under timed conditions)
  • After each mock — deep error analysis by section
  • Revise all landmark judgements and constitutional articles
  • Compile last 12 months' CA into a quick-revision booklet
  • Work on speed — track words per minute in reading
  • Solve at least 5 full past CLAT papers (2018–2025)
  • Attempt AILET, SLAT mock papers for additional practice

📅 Phase 4: Revision & Test Series (Dec 2026)

▼
  • Give a mock test every 2 days
  • Focus only on revision — no new topics
  • Review CA booklet daily in the last 2 weeks
  • Practice sectional tests — especially Legal Reasoning and GK
  • Sleep well and maintain routine — exam is typically in December
  • Re-read your notes for legal terms, constitutional articles, and important judgements
Ideal Daily Schedule (2–3 hrs/day alongside Class 12)

Morning (30 mins) — Newspaper Reading

Read The Hindu editorial + 2 national/international news. Note key points in CA diary.

Evening (60 mins) — Section Practice

Rotate: Mon/Wed = Legal Reasoning, Tue/Thu = Logical Reasoning, Fri = English RC, Sat = GK revision, Sun = Mock/Previous Year Paper

Evening (30 mins) — Quant & Vocabulary

10 minutes on math practice (2–3 problems), 10 minutes vocabulary review, 10 minutes for legal terms.

Weekend (2–3 hrs) — Mock / Full Sectional Test

One full section or full mock test. Dedicated error analysis session. Revise weak areas.

Attempt Strategy on Exam Day
  • Start with your strongest section to build confidence and save time
  • Recommended order: Legal → English → GK → Logical → Quant
  • Spend ~25 min on Legal, ~20 on English, ~25 on GK, ~20 on Logical, ~10 on Quant
  • Skip and return: If stuck, mark and move — don't waste 3 mins on one question
  • Negative marking: Only attempt if 70%+ confident. Skip if genuinely unsure.
  • Leave 5–7 minutes at the end to review flagged questions
